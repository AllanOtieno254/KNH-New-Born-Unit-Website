Immunization Support

Protecting newborns from preventable infections through timely, safe, and guided vaccination support.

View Vaccination Schedule

Why Immunization Matters for Newborns

Newborns have developing immune systems and are highly vulnerable to infections. Early vaccination protects babies from severe illnesses such as tuberculosis, hepatitis B, tetanus, and other life-threatening conditions.

  • Prevents severe diseases: Protects against infections common in early infancy.
  • Boosts immunity: Helps newborns develop strong defenses early.
  • Reduces hospital visits: Immunized babies have fewer complications later.
  • Required for medical records: Immunization cards support school and travel requirements.

Our Immunization Process

  • 1. Eligibility Screening: Nurse checks weight, age, stability, and any contraindications.
  • 2. Parental Counseling: Explanation of vaccine benefits, safety, and possible reactions.
  • 3. Vaccine Administration: Given safely by trained neonatal immunization nurses.
  • 4. Observation Period: Baby monitored for 15–30 minutes after vaccination.
  • 5. Documentation: Immunization card updated and entered into Kenya’s digital registry.
  • 6. Discharge Guidance: Parents taught how to care for the baby after vaccination.

    • Vaccines Given in the Newborn Unit

  • BCG (Tuberculosis): Protects against severe TB in infants, especially meningitis.
  • Hepatitis B (Birth Dose): Prevents early hepatitis B infection and chronic liver disease.
  • Oral Polio Vaccine (OPV Zero): Protects against poliomyelitis where applicable.
  • Vitamin K Injection: Prevents bleeding disorders in newborns (given to most babies).

    • Follow-Up Vaccination Schedule (After Discharge)

    • 6 Weeks: OPV 1, PCV10, Rotavirus 1, DPT-HepB-Hib
    • 10 Weeks: OPV 2, DPT-HepB-Hib 2, Rotavirus 2
    • 14 Weeks: OPV 3, DPT-HepB-Hib 3, PCV10 (second dose)
    • 9 Months: Measles-Rubella, Yellow Fever (where recommended)

    Vaccine Safety & What Parents Should Know

    All vaccines used at KNH NBU are approved by the Ministry of Health and follow global WHO safety guidelines. We ensure correct storage, preparation, dosage, and safe injection practices.

    Common Mild Reactions

    • Mild fever within 24 hours
    • Small swelling at injection site
    • Increased sleepiness or fussiness

    Seek medical help if you notice:

    • High fever lasting more than 48 hours
    • Severe swelling or redness
    • Difficulty breathing or unusual body movements

    How Parents Can Prepare

    • Feed your baby normally unless advised otherwise.
    • Carry the baby's Mother & Child Health (MCH) booklet.
    • Dress your baby in loose clothing for easy access.
    • Inform the nurse of any previous reactions or medical issues.
    • Plan to stay for 20–30 minutes for observation.

    FAQ — Immunization

    Is it safe to vaccinate small or premature babies?

    Yes. Premature babies often need vaccines more urgently. Nurses assess stability before giving any dose.

    Can my baby get all vaccines on the same day?

    Yes. Multiple vaccines can safely be administered at one visit without overloading the baby's immune system.

    What if my baby is sick during vaccination day?

    The nurse or pediatrician will assess. Mild illness is usually not a reason to delay vaccines.

    Will the injection be painful?

    A brief sting is normal. Babies can breastfeed or be held immediately after to comfort them.

    Where do I go for follow-up immunizations?

    You can return to KNH or visit any nearby government or accredited private facility offering RI services.
