Neonatal Intensive Care Unit (NICU)

Advanced, round-the-clock care for premature and critically ill newborns — clinical excellence, family-centred support, and modern neonatal technology.

Overview

The NICU at Kenyatta National Hospital provides specialized medical and nursing care to newborns who require intensive treatment. The unit admits preterm infants (born before 37 weeks), babies with low birth weight, newborns with respiratory distress, congenital anomalies that require monitoring or surgery, and infants with severe infections.

Mission in NICU

Deliver evidence-based neonatal care that maximizes survival and long-term wellbeing, while supporting families through clear communication, education, and compassionate care.

Capacity & Availability

The NICU operates 24/7 with a variable number of incubator and ventilator bays (check current capacity with staff). We have rotating consultant coverage, resident pediatricians, and specialist nurses at all times.

What We Offer

  • Respiratory support: CPAP, mechanical ventilation, oxygen therapy.
  • Thermoregulation: advanced incubators, warming systems, servo-control.
  • Nutrition & TPN: enteral feeding, parenteral nutrition for babies who cannot feed.
  • Phototherapy: high-efficiency phototherapy for neonatal jaundice.
  • Invasive monitoring: arterial lines, precise fluid & electrolyte management.
  • Neonatal resuscitation: immediate resuscitation and emergency procedures.

Equipment & Technology

Incubators / Thermoregulation systems

Maintain stable body temperature and allow close observation.

Ventilators & Respiratory Care

CPAP and mechanical ventilators with neonatal circuits and humidification.

Continuous Monitoring

Cardiorespiratory monitors, oxygen saturation, and digital charting systems.

Patient Journey: Admission → Care → Discharge

  1. Referral/Admission: Newborns may be referred from delivery ward, other hospitals, or arrive directly for emergency care. On arrival, triage and stabilization take place.
  2. Initial Assessment: Clinical examination, blood tests, blood glucose, cultures, and imaging (if indicated).
  3. Support & Treatment: Respiratory support, fluids/nutrition, antibiotics for infection, and specialized therapies as needed.
  4. Family Involvement: Parents receive daily updates, counsel on feeding, and invited to participate in kangaroo care when the infant is stable.
  5. Discharge Planning: The team provides guidance, vaccination schedule, outpatient follow-up, and homecare instructions.

Guidance for Parents

  • Visiting: Follow NICU visiting policy. Parents are prioritized; other visitors are limited for infection control.
  • Hygiene: Hand washing and use of gowns/masks when required.
  • Breastfeeding & Milk: Expressed breastmilk is encouraged; lactation specialists support pumping and storage.
  • Questions: Keep a notebook for questions and updates—ask the bedside nurse for daily briefings.

Frequently Asked Questions (NICU)

How long does a baby stay in the NICU?

Length of stay varies greatly depending on the baby's maturity and clinical condition—anywhere from a few days to several weeks or more. The team will give an estimate based on progress.

Can parents stay overnight?

Sleeping arrangements vary by hospital policy. KNH NBU prioritizes parental presence during the day and processes for extended access are handled case-by-case.

Is breastfeeding possible in NICU?

Yes—lactation support is available. Many NICU infants receive expressed breastmilk until they can feed directly.
